# Remote AI Career Roadmap — 8 Weeks (~1 hr/day alongside skill prep)

This runs **in parallel** with the [AI/ML Engineer roadmap](../ai-ml-engineer/roadmap.md) — ideally starting around its week 3, once you have 2 portfolio pieces. One focused hour a day: the job-search layer compounds while you build skills.

---

## Week 1 — Positioning: become inspectable

Remote employers hire what they can verify online.

- [ ] Rewrite your GitHub profile README: who you are, what you build, links to your best 3 projects — each project README must have: what it does, a screenshot/demo GIF, how to run it, and (for AI projects) an eval table. Recruiters spend 90 seconds; make them count.
- [ ] LinkedIn overhaul: headline = what you build, not "student" (e.g., "Building LLM apps — RAG, agents, evals | Python"); About section = 3 sentences + project links; turn on open-to-work for remote.
- [ ] Create accounts where remote AI hiring actually happens: [Wellfound](https://wellfound.com/), [Himalayas](https://himalayas.app/), [We Work Remotely](https://weworkremotely.com/), [Y Combinator's Work at a Startup](https://www.workatastartup.com/).
- [ ] Set up a simple one-page portfolio site (free: [GitHub Pages](https://pages.github.com/)) — projects, skills, contact. Nothing fancy; clarity wins.

**Milestone:** a stranger can go from your LinkedIn to a running demo of your best project in under 3 clicks.

## Week 2 — Calibrate the market

- [ ] Spend 3 days reading 30+ remote AI/ML job posts on the boards above. Build a spreadsheet: company, role, stack, seniority, salary (if listed), what they ask for. Patterns > opinions — this tells you what to emphasize.
- [ ] Check salary data so you negotiate from facts: [levels.fyi](https://www.levels.fyi/) for companies, board-listed ranges for startups. Note the going rate for "AI engineer, 0–2 yrs, remote".
- [ ] Read the monthly [Hacker News "Who is hiring?"](https://news.ycombinator.com/submitted?id=whoishiring) thread — search for "REMOTE" — this is where small well-funded startups post, often without a formal process.
- [ ] Write your baseline outreach materials: a 120-word cold email/DM template (who you are → one impressive specific thing → why them → ask), and a résumé that leads with projects, not education.

**Milestone:** target list of 30 companies/roles, sorted into A (dream), B (good), C (practice).

## Week 3–4 — Build the pipeline (volume + quality)

- [ ] Apply to 5 roles/week minimum — start with C-list (practice), move to B/A as your materials improve. Tailor the first 2 résumé lines per application; don't hand-craft everything else.
- [ ] Track every application in your spreadsheet: date, stage, follow-up date. Follow up once after 7 days — politely; it genuinely moves the needle.
- [ ] Freelance track (Route B): create an [Upwork](https://www.upwork.com/) profile scoped to ONE offer ("I build AI chatbots / document-Q&A systems for small businesses"). Bid on 5 small jobs/week with a specific 3-sentence proposal referencing their actual problem. Expect 10–20 bids before the first win — the first review is the hard one.
- [ ] Start one public artifact: a short write-up of your RAG + evals project on [dev.to](https://dev.to/) or [Hashnode](https://hashnode.com/) — remote offers disproportionately come through being findable.

**Milestone:** 10+ applications out, 10+ freelance bids out, 1 article published.

## Week 5–6 — Network like an engineer (not a spammer)

- [ ] Open source: pick one AI tool you actually use ([MCP servers](https://modelcontextprotocol.io/), LangChain, a vector DB client) and land 2 small PRs — good first issues, docs fixes, small bugs. "Contributor to X" is remote-credibility currency.
- [ ] 10 warm-ish DMs/emails per week: to engineers (not recruiters) at your A-list companies — ask one specific question about their work, not for a job. A fraction respond; some become referrals. Referral applications convert ~5–10× better than cold ones.
- [ ] Join where remote AI people hang out: relevant Discords (framework communities), X/Twitter AI-builder circles. Share what you're building; ask good questions.
- [ ] If Route C is live (local interviews), run them with the full [AI/ML interview prep](../ai-ml-engineer/interview-prep.md) — a local offer is negotiation leverage everywhere else.

**Milestone:** 2 merged PRs, 3 real conversations with engineers at target companies.

## Week 7–8 — Convert: interviews and offers

- [ ] Drill the remote-specific rounds in [interview-prep.md](interview-prep.md): async communication test, take-home discipline, "why remote/why you" answers, timezone questions.
- [ ] Rehearse the negotiation scripts (same file) out loud — the first unprepared "what are your salary expectations?" costs real money.
- [ ] Take-homes: treat every one like the week-4 milestone of the AI/ML roadmap — working code, README, eval table, short video walkthrough. Most candidates skip the video; it's the single cheapest differentiator.
- [ ] Keep the pipeline full: 5 applications + 5 bids/week continues until you sign. Pipelines die silently the week you stop feeding them.

**Milestone:** first offers in hand — negotiate (scripts ready), and compare *total* value: salary + currency + growth + whether the work builds your next jump.

---

## After week 8 — the compounding loop

The 8 weeks build a machine; keep it running at ~30 min/day: 5 applications/week, 1 public write-up/month, open-source contributions when natural, spreadsheet always current. Typical fresher timelines to a signed remote/high-pay offer are 2–5 months of consistent pipeline — the people who fail mostly stopped in month 2.

And re-read the honest math in the [README](README.md) monthly: if Route C (local first) produces a strong offer and Routes A/B haven't, **take it** — 18 months later you re-enter the remote market as mid-level, where the $60–100k offers actually live.
