# Remote Hiring: Interviews, Negotiation, Red Flags

Remote loops test everything the normal loops test (use the [AI/ML interview prep](../ai-ml-engineer/interview-prep.md) for the technical rounds) **plus** an async-trust layer covered here.

---

## The typical remote-startup loop

1. **Application + screening questions** — often written; your answers *are* an async-communication test
2. **Recruiter/founder call** (30 min) — motivation, timezone, salary range (be ready — scripts below)
3. **Take-home** (2–6 hrs) — the highest-weight stage at most remote startups; replaces or precedes live coding
4. **Technical interview** (60–90 min) — take-home deep-dive + live problem
5. **Collaboration/culture round** — async habits, communication, self-management
6. **Offer + negotiation**

Startups run this in 1–3 weeks. Silence for 2+ weeks usually means no — keep the pipeline full and move on.

## Remote-specific Q&A (with answers)

### ⭐ "Why should we hire someone remote and junior?"
**Answer pattern:** don't argue the category — show you're the exception with evidence: "The usual risk with junior remote hires is that they need synchronous hand-holding. Here's my counter-evidence: I've shipped [project] end-to-end with written documentation good enough that you can run it without me in the room; my take-home included an eval table and a video walkthrough because I default to making my work inspectable; and I've [freelance client / open-source PRs] — delivered to people who only judged me on output. You'll see my work in the repo and my thinking in the PR descriptions, daily."

### ⭐ "How do you work when nobody's watching?"
**Answer pattern:** describe a *system*, not a virtue: "Written plan each morning (3 outcomes, not tasks); work in public — draft PRs early, progress notes in the channel before anyone asks; timebox blockers — 45 minutes of honest effort, then I post a specific question with what I tried. The rule I hold myself to: nobody should ever have to ask me 'what's the status?'"

### ⭐ "What's your timezone overlap / how do you handle async collaboration?"
**Answer:** know your overlap math before the call (IST: ~4 good hours with EU mornings; US East ~7:30 pm–11 pm IST; US West is hard — say honestly what you can sustain). Then: "I front-load communication so overlap hours are for decisions, not status: questions written with context and my proposed answer, so a reviewer in another timezone can unblock me with one message. I've read how [GitLab's handbook](https://handbook.gitlab.com/) treats async as the default — that's how I like to work."

### "What salary are you expecting?" (the trap question)
See the negotiation section below — never answer with a number first if you can avoid it, and never anchor in your local currency for a global role.

### "Have you worked remotely before?"
**Answer pattern (for a fresher):** reframe to remote-shaped evidence: "Not employed remotely yet — but my open-source contributions were reviewed and merged by maintainers I've never met, my freelance clients judged me purely on delivered work, and my projects are documented so strangers can run them. That's the remote workflow: written communication, inspectable output, no supervision."

## Take-home rules (where remote offers are actually won)

1. **Confirm scope and timebox in writing** before starting ("I'll spend ~5 hours and prioritize X over Y — sound right?"). This is itself a signal.
2. Working > clever. A boring solution that runs from README instructions beats a brilliant one that doesn't.
3. **Always include:** README (what/how/why), tests or an eval table, a "what I'd do with more time" section (shows judgment without doing the work), and a **5-minute video walkthrough** (Loom or plain screen recording) — almost nobody does this; it humanizes you and proves communication skill in one artifact.
4. Deliver *early* if you can — remote teams notice reliability signals more than brilliance signals.

## Salary negotiation (scripts — rehearse out loud)

### The rules
- **Never give the first number** if avoidable; if forced, give a researched *range* anchored to the role's market, not your local market or current pay.
- **Currency framing:** for global-remote roles, speak in USD annual. Anchoring in INR invites local-market pricing for global-market work.
- **Your leverage is alternatives**, not arguments — which is why the roadmap keeps three routes running. One competing offer changes everything.
- Negotiate **after** the offer, never during screening enthusiasm-checks.

### Script 1 — deflecting the early "salary expectations?" question
> "I'd rather make sure there's a mutual fit first — comp-wise I'm confident we won't be far apart. Could you share the budgeted range for this role?"

If they insist: give a range from your Week-2 research: "Based on what I'm seeing for remote AI engineering at this level, something in the $X–$Y range, depending on the overall package."

### Script 2 — responding to the offer (never accept on the call)
> "Thank you — I'm genuinely excited about this. Let me review the full package and get back to you by [day]."

Then, with any competing data point:
> "I'm ready to say yes at $X. [If true:] I'm also in process elsewhere at similar numbers, but this role is my first choice because [specific real reason]. Can we get there?"

### Script 3 — when the number is firm
Negotiate the non-salary levers, in rough order of long-term value: **6-month review with defined raise criteria in writing** → equity → learning budget/conference → extra leave. The written 6-month review is the fresher's best lever: it converts "prove yourself" into a contract.

### Contractor vs employee (know before you negotiate)
Global startups hire internationally as **contractors** (you invoice monthly, handle your own taxes — common, legitimate) or via **EOR platforms** (Deel/Remote.com — you get local-law employment, benefits, but the company pays platform fees, so all-in budgets shrink slightly). Contractor rate math: a $60k employee package ≈ $65–70k contractor equivalent (you're covering benefits/taxes) — it's normal to ask for more as a contractor, and normal for them to expect it.

## Red flags & scams (remote job seekers are targets)

- **Any "job" asking you to pay** — for equipment, training, deposits, "processing": scam, always.
- Interviews only over chat, offer without any technical evaluation, pressure to accept in 24 hours: scam patterns.
- **Unpaid "trial projects"** beyond a reasonable take-home (>1 day of work, or work suspiciously identical to their real product): decline politely or ask for a paid trial — legitimate startups pay for trials.
- Salary "in crypto," employer asking for your bank login, or checks to "deposit and forward": run.
- Verify companies: real website + real employees on LinkedIn + posted on a reputable board. When in doubt, search "[company] scam" and check their domain age.
